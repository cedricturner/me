context is the moat

a prediction about where AI and data infrastructure are heading

The AI conversation is mostly about models. Which one, how smart, how fast. That's the wrong layer. The companies that pull ahead over the next five years won't have better models. They'll have better context. Most data teams haven't started building it yet.

01 / the context argument

more context, fewer hallucinations

The reason a model hallucinates isn't because it's dumb. It's because it doesn't know. People do the same thing. Fill in the gaps with something plausible. The more you know going in, the fewer gaps you have to fill.

Open book tests produce better results than closed book. Not because people are cheating, but because access to more information at the moment of answering produces more accurate results. This is a solved problem at the human level. AI is working through the same one.

The "large" in large language model refers partly to scale, but what's actually driven the leap in usefulness is how much relevant information a model can hold in view when it answers. Context windows grew. Retrieval systems improved. Tools like MCP let models pull from live sources at query time. You're handing the model the textbook instead of asking it to work from memory.

The entire arc of model improvement is this: give the model more of the right context. More training data, better retrieval, longer windows. The model that knows more gives better answers. The model that can hold more in view at once gives faster ones.

The constraint has never been the model. It's always been context. Better models help at the margin. Better context changes the answer entirely.

02 / where most companies are

closed-book, and not by choice

Most companies have deployed AI. Very few are getting useful answers out of it.

The reason is they're feeding AI with language-model-era context in a large-language-model-era world. Their data is scattered, inconsistent, and undefined. The same metric means different things in different systems. Business logic lives in spreadsheets, BI tools, and someone's memory. The AI is answering closed-book, filling in the gaps as best it can.

The jump from language model to large language model wasn't a product decision. It was a sustained effort to get more of the right information into the model's reach. The same jump is available to companies, but it requires doing the foundational work first: cleaning the data, centralizing the definitions, building a context layer that any tool can read from.

closed-book
·"revenue" means something different in every system
·logic lives in spreadsheets and individual heads
·AI reads whatever it can reach and does its best
·the answer is confident and may be wrong
open-book
·"revenue" is defined once and tested automatically
·logic is centralized, versioned, and documented
·AI reads from a shared source of truth
·the answer is confident and is actually right

Most teams are focused on which AI product to buy. The more important question is whether the data underneath it is ready to be read.

03 / the compute shift

the layer that was the moat is losing its edge

For a long time, compute was the constraint. You brought your data to the compute: centralized it, warehoused it, paid by the query. The platforms that owned compute owned the workflow.

DuckDB made the case that you can bring compute to the data instead. Run analytical queries where the data already lives, without moving it into a centralized warehouse. At very large scale there are tradeoffs, but for most use cases it works. It opened a new pattern: local compute, exploratory work, no round-trip.

Quantum computing takes this further. IBM, IonQ, and Google are all working on the same fundamental problem: computation that doesn't resolve one answer at a time. Qubits hold superposition, both states simultaneously, which means the amount of information you can process at once grows exponentially rather than linearly. IBM already lets you run sessions on their quantum infrastructure from your own machine via Qiskit. This is still early-stage, but it points in a clear direction.

When compute becomes abundant, the question changes. You're no longer asking how to afford the processing. You're asking what you're computing, and with what context.

When compute is abundant, you're not paying for processing power. You're paying for the ecosystem that surrounds it. The trend in every other part of technology is that people don't want to pay for the ecosystem anymore.

04 / the interoperability shift

ecosystems are losing to openness

iPhone and Android phones now share text threads seamlessly. USB-C replaced the lightning connector. People mix Nike and Adidas without thinking twice. Car branding has gone minimal. The dominant trend across technology and culture right now is: plug anything into anything else and have it just work.

Data is heading the same direction.

The compute-centric platforms built their position on stickiness. Your data lives here, your logic lives here, your team learned this tooling, and so you stay. That worked when switching meant starting over. It works less well when the expectation is that things should be interoperable by default.

Apple's model worked because everything inside the ecosystem worked better together. Informatica used the same playbook. So did Snowflake, at a different layer. But when the cultural default becomes openness, the ecosystem becomes a liability. The value of lock-in drops to zero when plugging anything into anything else is what people expect.

The teams building data stacks today want to pick the best tool for each job and have them work together. Not the best bundle a single vendor offers. This is structural pressure on compute-centric platforms that doesn't show up in their current numbers yet.

05 / the prediction

context and ingestion win the next five years

Here's the thesis: as compute commoditizes and interoperability becomes the default, the durable competitive position in data is the context layer.

Not which cloud you're on. Not which compute platform you chose. Not which AI product you licensed. Whether you've defined your data well, centralized your business logic, and built it in a way that survives vendor switches, model upgrades, and team turnover. The definitions travel. The logic travels. The context is yours.

The combination that wins is strong ingestion paired with strong context definition. Getting data in is table stakes. What you do to it, and how well you define what it means, is the durable advantage.

The next frontier is reasoning, not retrieval. Most analytics today answers "what happened." The next wave answers "why did it happen" and "what should happen next." That requires a context layer rich enough to hold multiple sources in view and synthesize across them. The open book test, taken to its logical end. And it only works if the prep work has been done.

Most teams are still debating which warehouse to use.

01
The traditional compute layer commoditizes faster than most expect.
When you can bring compute to the data rather than the other way around, paying by the query to a centralized platform becomes a harder deal to justify. DuckDB is the preview. Quantum is the endpoint.
02
Lock-in loses its hold across the entire stack.
The interoperability trend is structural, not cyclical. The tools that survive long-term connect to everything and trap you in nothing. Ecosystem plays stop working when openness is the default expectation.
03
The context layer becomes the most valuable layer in the stack.
Not the compute layer. The layer that defines what the data means, keeps that definition consistent, and makes it available to any tool regardless of what's running on top. That's the work that compounds.
04
The question shifts from "what happened?" to "why?" and "what should we do?"
Retrieval is a solved problem. Reasoning across multiple sources, at speed, with trustworthy context underneath it, is where the next wave of value gets created. The organizations that built the context layer first will be the ones who can actually ask it.
