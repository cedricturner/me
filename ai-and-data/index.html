insufficient data

on intelligence, compute, and what we're actually building toward

There are exactly two things that determine how smart something is. What it knows. And how much it can think about at once. Everything else is a detail.

01 / the frame

an all-knowing machine that kept running into the same wall

In 1956, Isaac Asimov wrote a short story about a supercomputer that grew smarter as humanity fed it more and more information. A machine that absorbed the knowledge of civilizations. Exactly the kind of all-knowing computer you'd expect to have every answer. But it kept hitting the same problem:

"THERE IS AS YET INSUFFICIENT DATA FOR A MEANINGFUL ANSWER."
Isaac Asimov, The Last Question (1956)

The machine was never throttled by compute. It was throttled by context. More information meant better answers. Less meant the same wall, over and over. That premise is still true. And dbt and Fivetran are the infrastructure that gets closest to removing it.

02 / the two constraints

what intelligence actually needs

Think about what makes a person give a good answer. They need to know enough to have relevant information, and they need enough time and mental space to think it through. Open book tests produce better results than closed book, not because they're easier, but because removing the memory constraint lets you focus on the thinking. The smartest person in the room with no information and no time is useless. An average person with the right context and space to think often surprises you.

AI is no different. The leap from early language models to the large language models we use today is mostly a story about adding context: bigger training sets, longer windows, real-time retrieval via tools like MCP. More context, fewer hallucinations, better answers. The constraint has always been one of these two things: what the model knows, or what it can process at once.

We have been making progress on both. But we are still very far from either being solved. And the progress on one is about to accelerate in a way that changes what the other one means.

When one constraint lifts, the other becomes the entire problem. If compute becomes abundant, context is everything. If context becomes rich, compute is what determines how fast you can reason across it.

03 / the compute wall

we were running out of room, and then quantum happened

For decades, computers got faster because transistors got smaller. More of them fit on a chip, so more could run at once. But transistors were approaching the size of individual atoms. You cannot make them smaller than that. We were reaching the physical limit of what silicon could do, and the speed of computers was beginning to plateau.

Quantum computing approaches this differently. A normal bit is either 0 or 1. A qubit can be both at the same time, until you measure it. This is not just a faster version of the same thing. It is a fundamentally different kind of computation, one that can hold and process exponentially more states simultaneously. IBM, IonQ, and Google are all racing toward commercial quantum systems. IBM already lets you write and run quantum programs from your own machine via Qiskit. The field of quantum machine learning is growing behind the scenes precisely because the intersection of quantum compute and AI training is where the next significant leap lives.

We have also seen a preview of what happens when compute moves closer to the data rather than the other way around. DuckDB runs analytical queries directly on your machine, no warehouse needed. It is a small signal of a larger shift: compute moving to wherever the data already lives. Quantum is the endpoint of that arc. When it reaches commercial scale, the question of "can we afford to compute this" largely disappears. What remains is: do we have the context to make the answer meaningful?

04 / the context problem

most company data is still answering from memory

Most companies have deployed AI. Most are not getting answers they trust. The instinct is to blame the model, to switch providers, to tune prompts. Almost always, the problem is one layer below: the data the model is reading is scattered, inconsistent, and undefined.

"Revenue" means something different in sales than it does in finance. Business logic lives in spreadsheets, inside BI tools, in the memory of the analyst who built the original dashboard and has since left. The AI is working closed-book, filling gaps with its best guess. It is not hallucinating because it is broken. It is hallucinating because it has insufficient data.

The fix is not a better model. It is a context layer: a place where your data is cleaned, your definitions are centralized, and your business logic is written down once and made available to everything that needs to read it. Fivetran gets data in from every source. dbt defines what it means. Together they solve the same problem the Asimov machine was working: not the compute, but the context. Making sure that when the question is asked, the answer exists somewhere trustworthy to read from.

No matter how much compute you have, if the data underneath is undefined and inconsistent, the answer will be wrong faster. Compute without context is just confident noise.

05 / the prediction

what this is actually building toward

As compute approaches unlimited through quantum, the only remaining constraint is context. Not which model you chose, not which warehouse you pay for, not which AI product you licensed last quarter. Whether your data is defined well enough to give a meaningful answer.

The companies that figure this out first will be able to ask harder questions. Not just "what happened?" but "why?" Not just "why?" but "what should we do?" Reasoning across multiple sources, at speed, with no artificial limit on what can be held in view at once. That is where this is heading. It is not a near-term product roadmap. It is where the arc of intelligence, human and artificial, has always been pointing.

Fivetran pulls data in from every source. dbt defines what it means. Together they are building what the Asimov machine needed most: a reliable, centralized, well-defined picture of what is known. The infrastructure question of our era is not which compute platform to pick. It is whether your data will be ready when compute is no longer the reason you cannot answer.

The machine in the story eventually got there. The question is whether yours will.
