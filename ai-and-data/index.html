what AI actually runs on

for anyone in a conversation about AI + data

AI isn't magic. It reads data. And if the data underneath it is inconsistent, undefined, or scattered across a dozen systems, the AI will be too. This is a quick explanation of what's actually going on, and how to talk about it.

01 / the shift

the interface changed. the problem didn't.

A few years ago, when someone had a question about the business, they'd pull up a dashboard or ask an analyst to build one.

Now they're asking AI directly, in plain English, and they expect an answer in seconds.

The interface changed, but the data underneath still has to answer the question and still has to be right. That's the part most people skip over.

then
·someone has a question
·analyst writes SQL
·dashboard shows the answer
·person decides
now
·someone has a question
·asks AI in plain language
·AI reads data and responds
·person decides

The middle steps look different, but they still depend on the same thing: data that is clean, defined, and trustworthy.

02 / the system

there is a system. most people only see the top of it.

Think of it like a restaurant kitchen. Before a chef can cook, someone has to bring in the ingredients, do all the prep work, and write out the recipes. The chef, no matter how talented, can only work with what's in front of them.

Data works the same way. There are four layers, and AI sits at the top of them.

raw data

scattered across every system: CRM, databases, spreadsheets, third-party tools. Rarely consistent or labeled.

Most conversations about AI are about the chef. They're impressive. But a fast chef with bad ingredients still makes bad food.

The companies getting real value from AI aren't necessarily using better models. They've done the prep work the others haven't.

03 / where things break

most AI problems are actually data problems.

When AI gives a bad answer, the instinct is to blame the model, switch to a different one, tune the prompts, or try a different product. But usually that's not where the problem is.

scenario 1 · conflicting definitions

Sales defines revenue as bookings. Finance defines it as recognized revenue. The AI gets asked for "revenue." It picks one definition, or worse, it tries to reconcile two inconsistent sources. Nobody agrees with the answer.

The AI isn't wrong. The company never agreed on what "revenue" meant.

scenario 2 · logic scattered everywhere

Churn is calculated slightly differently in Salesforce, in the data warehouse, and in the BI tool. The AI pulls from whichever one is easiest to reach. The number it returns is technically from the data, but nobody can explain how it got there.

The AI did exactly what it was supposed to. The system underneath it wasn't consistent.

scenario 3 · answers that don't match the dashboard

An exec asks AI to summarize performance. The numbers don't match the BI dashboard. Both are reading the same underlying data. Everyone loses confidence in both.

Two systems reading the same raw data can produce different answers if neither of them agree on definitions.

These aren't AI problems, they're data shape problems. The AI just made them more visible.

04 / where dbt fits

dbt is the prep kitchen.

dbt sits between raw data and everything that uses it. Its job is to take scattered, inconsistent source data and turn it into something reliable.

It's not a BI tool or a database, it doesn't store your data or show you charts. It does one thing: makes raw data usable and trustworthy.

without dbt
·"revenue" means different things in different systems
·business logic lives in spreadsheets, BI tools, and someone's head
·AI reads whatever it can find and does its best
·the answer is fast and confident, and may be wrong
with dbt
·"revenue" has one definition, tested automatically
·business logic lives in one place, versioned and documented
·AI reads what dbt made trustworthy
·the answer is fast and confident, and is actually right

AI doesn't write its own definitions, it reads the ones that already exist. dbt is where those definitions get written and enforced, and that's why it matters.

There's a second layer to this that matters a lot: the semantic layer. If transformation is the prep kitchen, the semantic layer is where you write the recipe cards — the standardized instructions that any chef can follow and get the same dish every time. dbt's semantic layer lets you define "revenue" or "churn" once, centrally, and then broadcast that definition to every tool that needs it. Your BI dashboard reads it. Your AI agent reads it. A data product reads it. They all get the same answer because they're all reading the same recipe card.

This is what prevents the scenario where your AI and your dashboard disagree. It's not a model problem. It's a definitions problem, and the semantic layer is the fix.

When someone buys an AI product, they're buying the chef, and dbt is the prep kitchen and the recipe book. The recipe book is what stops two chefs from making the same dish two different ways.

05 / how to talk about this

lines you can actually use.

These are reframes and openers, not scripts. Use whichever ones fit the conversation.

for AEs · pull these out as needed
  • "You don't have an AI problem. You have a data trust problem."
    Use this when they're frustrated that AI gives them different answers to the same question. It reframes where the real fix needs to happen.
  • "AI is only as smart as the data it's reading. Right now, is that data defined the same way everywhere?"
    Good discovery question. Most people pause here. Opens a real conversation about where their definitions live.
  • "Think of AI like a really fast new hire. If you give them bad instructions, they execute bad instructions really fast."
    Works with any audience, technical or not. Immediately makes data quality feel urgent instead of abstract.
  • "When your AI and your dashboard show different numbers, that's not an AI problem. That's a definitions problem."
    Very specific. If they've experienced this (they almost always have), it lands immediately.
  • "Most companies are trying to build the AI layer before they've built the data layer. dbt is the data layer."
    Positions dbt as the foundation, not an add-on. Changes the framing from "do we need this?" to "we need this first."
  • "Every AI vendor is selling you the chef. dbt is the prep kitchen. You need both."
    Use after you've explained the system. Makes the kitchen analogy stick and makes the dependency clear.
  • "dbt's semantic layer means 'revenue' is defined once. Your dashboard reads it. Your AI reads it. They match."
    Use when they've experienced AI and dashboards giving different numbers. Makes the semantic layer concrete without getting into the technical details.
  • "The question isn't whether you're using AI. It's what your AI is actually reading."
    Good for executive conversations where the focus is on AI adoption. Shifts attention to the foundation underneath.
  • "Where does 'revenue' live in your system right now? How many places is it defined?"
    Ask this and wait. If they can't answer quickly, the problem is real. This is discovery, not a pitch.
