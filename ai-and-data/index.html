the data problem AI can't solve

on what AI actually needs, and who's building it

Intelligence has two constraints: how much you know and how much you can think about at once.

01
context
what you know, and how complete and trustworthy that knowledge is
02
compute
how much you can process at once, and how fast you can find the answer

Artificial intelligence is still intelligence. The same two constraints apply. The race toward something like AC is not just a race for better models. It is a race to build the memory layer: the combination of context and compute that makes real intelligence possible. Better compute is coming. The context side is on you.

01 / AC

what we're chasing

In 1956, long before anyone had named artificial intelligence, Isaac Asimov imagined a machine that would outlast civilizations. He called it AC. Not a tool you queried and closed, but something that grew with the world, absorbed the memory of every generation that came before it, got smarter as humanity fed it more context, more history, more of everything the species had ever known. AC never refused a question. That was not in its nature. What AC refused to do was guess. When it did not have enough to give a real answer, it said so, directly and without apology, every single time:

"THERE IS AS YET INSUFFICIENT DATA FOR A MEANINGFUL ANSWER."
Isaac Asimov, The Last Question (1956)

That honesty is what makes AC worth paying attention to. It had access to more accumulated knowledge than any individual could build in a lifetime and still had the self-awareness to know when that was not enough. It was not broken, it was not failing, it was being precise about what it actually knew versus what it would have had to make up. Asimov wrote this before machine learning existed, before anyone had named the problem he was describing, before computers could do much of anything at all. He just saw the logic: AC is only as smart as what it knows, and the smarter you want it to be, the more complete and trustworthy that knowledge has to be. That logic has not changed. We are just now living inside it.

02 / the two constraints

what intelligence actually is

Think about the last time you were asked a question you only half-knew the answer to. You gave the best answer you could with what you had, filling in the gaps with your best guess, and if you were wrong nobody could blame you because you were working with what you had. That is exactly what AI does too, and it is exactly why AC kept getting the same answer: not enough to go on.

How smart something is comes down to two things, what it knows and what it can process at once, and the entire history of making AI better is a story about pushing on both of those levers. Open book tests produce better results than closed book, not because they are easier, but because removing the memory constraint lets you focus on the reasoning itself. Tools like MCP exist for exactly this reason, giving the model access to live information at the moment it needs to answer, more context, fewer hallucinations, better answers. The large language models we use today are not smarter than earlier ones because of magic, they are smarter because they have access to more.

AC had both of these constraints too, and it kept pushing on both of them. So are we.

03 / the compute side

supercompute is on the way

For decades, making computers faster meant making their parts smaller, packing more of them onto a chip so more could run at the same time. It worked remarkably well, until the parts got so small they were approaching the size of individual atoms, and there is simply nowhere smaller to go. The speed of traditional computers was beginning to plateau.

Quantum computing takes a completely different approach. The best way to understand it is through a decision. Imagine you are playing a game where every choice you make changes the outcome, and you need to find the winning path. A traditional computer tries one choice, sees what happens, backtracks if it was wrong, tries the next one, and so on until it finds the answer, one path at a time. A quantum computer can take every path simultaneously, run every possible outcome at once, and find the right answer without the trial and error.

↓ wrong, go back

↓ wrong, go back

↓ right
found it (eventually)
quantum compute
every path at once

try path B
try path C
↓ all at once
found it (instantly)

IBM, IonQ, Google, Microsoft, and a growing wave of startups are all racing toward commercial quantum systems, mostly out of the public eye because there is no commercial use case to ship yet. The breakthroughs are happening in labs, not product launches. But that is exactly how transformative technology tends to move, quietly, until it does not. Quantum is best understood as the next major acceleration layer: the way NVIDIA's GPUs and the data centers built around them reshaped what AI could process, applied to a different class of problems. Anything that benefits from evaluating many possibilities at once gets dramatically faster. It will not instantly replace every piece of existing infrastructure, but when it reaches commercial scale it will raise the ceiling on how much a model can reason through at a time. Quantum is the path to supercompute: the kind of compute that does not just move faster, but processes every possibility at once.

Quantum may raise the ceiling on what machines can reason through. But it does not fix whether the information they reason over is true, complete, governed, or meaningful. When compute gets faster, the context problem does not get easier. It gets more exposed.

04 / the other side

supercompute is not the end

When compute gets cheaper and faster, moving and processing data gets easier. That part has been true through every wave of infrastructure change, from on-premise to cloud to serverless. The plumbing gets simpler. You need fewer people to operate it and less time to maintain it.

But the plumbing was never the hard part. The hard part is whether the data being moved means anything: whether "revenue" in the warehouse matches what finance means when they say it, whether a number an agent surfaces can be traced back to something real.

Those problems do not get easier when compute accelerates. They get more consequential. An agent with faster reasoning, working from undefined or inconsistent data, does not produce better answers. It produces wrong answers faster.

Data engineering does not disappear. It moves up the stack: from plumbing and optimization toward something closer to context engineering, structuring information so the systems consuming it can reason over it, trust it, and trace it.

Call it supercontext: the yin to supercompute's yang. Supercompute is the capacity to process everything at once. Supercontext is having everything worth processing. In an AC system, that means every source connected and streaming with no gaps in what AC can see, every metric defined and tested so AC reads the same answer regardless of where it looks, full lineage so every output can be traced back to its origin, and shared definitions that hold across every team and domain feeding the stack. Not more data. The right data, fully structured, fully governed, and fully traceable. Supercompute without supercontext is just a faster way to be confidently wrong. Both have to scale together.

05 / the context side

supercontext

Supercontext is not just good data. It is information organized to a standard that a reasoning system can navigate without introducing contradictions. The bar is higher than it sounds. When supercompute evaluates thousands of scenarios at once, inconsistencies in the underlying context do not cancel each other out. They multiply. A wrong definition does not produce one wrong answer. It produces thousands of them, all confident, all at once.

Think of it in two layers. The first is about address: every piece of information needs a known location, a defined meaning, a traceable origin. Not just stored somewhere, but registered. You can find it, you know exactly what it counts, and you can follow it back to where it came from. Without this, information exists but nothing is findable. With it, any question has a path to an answer.

The second layer is what sits on top of that foundation: the intelligence that gives information meaning in context. Think of the ultimate librarian: not just knowing where every book is, but understanding which books answer which questions, how the ideas inside them connect, and what answer emerges when you pull them together. That is what AC reads from: not raw data that lives somewhere, but structured, navigable knowledge that a reasoning system can move through without losing the thread. The semantic layer is the vocabulary. The lineage graph is the map. The first layer makes it findable. The second makes it thinkable.

Without both, AC has no address for truth. It reads whatever it can find and resolves conflicts with its best guess.

06 / the agent era

the stakes just got higher

Most people think of AI as something that answers questions. Ask it something, it responds, you decide what to do with the answer. That version of AI is already being replaced.

AI agents do not just answer, they act. An agent does not tell you what your pipeline looks like, it goes and checks it, finds the anomaly, cross-references it against three other data sources, and either flags it or fixes it depending on what it was told to do. The difference between a wrong answer and a wrong action is the difference between something you can correct and something you have to clean up. When an agent is working from bad data, it does not just produce a wrong answer with confidence, it takes a wrong action with confidence, and that is a much harder thing to recover from.

This is where most companies are unprepared. They have invested in AI, they have the models, they are deploying agents, and the agents are working from the same scattered, inconsistent, undefined data that the dashboards were always working from. The tolerance for that kind of data was low before. For agents, it is near zero.

AC was not a question-answering system. It was an agent at cosmic scale, one that could act on everything it knew. The thing that kept it from its answer was never a lack of willingness to act. It was insufficient data. That is still the problem. It is just more urgent now.

08 / the prediction

when both constraints lift

Supercompute is being built. The labs are ahead of schedule. When quantum computing reaches commercial scale, the first constraint on AC lifts: processing is no longer the ceiling. What is left is supercontext. The companies spending the next few years connecting every source, defining every metric, and governing every output are the ones who will be ready when compute catches up. Not just to ask harder questions. To actually get answers: not "what happened?" with caveats, but "what should we do next?" with a full chain of reasoning you can trace all the way back.

the convergence

Supercompute without supercontext produces confident wrong answers, faster. Supercontext without supercompute produces right answers, slowly. When both constraints lift at once, you get something that has not existed before: a system that can reason over everything, trust what it is reading, and answer without guessing. That is AC. Not a product. A direction. And the infrastructure to build it exists now.
