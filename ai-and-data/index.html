what AI actually runs on

for anyone in a conversation about AI + data

AI isn't magic. It reads data. And if the data underneath it is inconsistent, undefined, or scattered across a dozen systems, the AI will be too. This is a quick explanation of what's actually going on, and how to talk about it.

01 / the shift

the interface changed, the problem didn't

A few years ago, when someone had a question about the business, they'd pull up a dashboard or ask an analyst to build one.

Now they're asking AI directly, in plain English, and they expect an answer in seconds.

The interface changed, but the data underneath still has to answer the question and still has to be right. That's the part most people skip over.

then
·someone has a question
·analyst writes SQL
·dashboard shows the answer
·person decides
now
·someone has a question
·asks AI in plain language
·AI reads data and responds
·person decides

The middle steps look different, but they still depend on the same thing: data that is clean, defined, and trustworthy.

02 / the system

there is a system, most people only see the top of it

Think of it like a restaurant kitchen. Before a chef can cook, someone has to bring in the ingredients, do all the prep work, and write out the recipes. The chef, no matter how talented, can only work with what's in front of them.

Data works the same way. There are four layers, and AI sits at the top of them.

raw data

scattered across every system: CRM, databases, spreadsheets, third-party tools. Rarely consistent or labeled.

Most conversations about AI are about the chef. They're impressive. But a fast chef with bad ingredients still makes bad food.

The companies getting real value from AI aren't necessarily using better models. They've done the prep work the others haven't.

03 / where things break

most AI problems are actually data problems

When AI gives a bad answer, the instinct is to blame the model, switch to a different one, tune the prompts, or try a different product. But usually that's not where the problem is.

scenario 1 · conflicting definitions

Sales defines revenue as bookings. Finance defines it as recognized revenue. The AI gets asked for "revenue." It picks one definition, or worse, it tries to reconcile two inconsistent sources. Nobody agrees with the answer.

The AI isn't wrong. The company never agreed on what "revenue" meant.

scenario 2 · logic scattered everywhere

Churn is calculated slightly differently in Salesforce, in the data warehouse, and in the BI tool. The AI pulls from whichever one is easiest to reach. The number it returns is technically from the data, but nobody can explain how it got there.

The AI did exactly what it was supposed to. The system underneath it wasn't consistent.

scenario 3 · answers that don't match the dashboard

An exec asks AI to summarize performance. The numbers don't match the BI dashboard. Both are reading the same underlying data. Everyone loses confidence in both.

Two systems reading the same raw data can produce different answers if neither of them agree on definitions.

These aren't AI problems, they're data shape problems. The AI just made them more visible.

04 / where dbt fits

dbt is the prep kitchen

dbt sits between raw data and everything that uses it. Its job is to take scattered, inconsistent source data and turn it into something reliable.

It's not a BI tool or a database, it doesn't store your data or show you charts. It does one thing: makes raw data usable and trustworthy.

without dbt
·"revenue" means different things in different systems
·business logic lives in spreadsheets, BI tools, and someone's head
·AI reads whatever it can find and does its best
·the answer is fast and confident, and may be wrong
with dbt
·"revenue" has one definition, tested automatically
·business logic lives in one place, versioned and documented
·AI reads what dbt made trustworthy
·the answer is fast and confident, and is actually right

AI doesn't write its own definitions, it reads the ones that already exist. dbt is where those definitions get written and enforced, and that's why it matters.

There's a second layer to this that matters a lot: the semantic layer. If transformation is the prep kitchen, the semantic layer is where you write the recipe cards. Think about what happens when every chef uses their own recipe. The dish is called the same thing on the menu, but it tastes different every time depending on who's working that night. Customers notice. Trust breaks down. Now replace the chefs with your tools: a dashboard, an AI agent, a data product. If each one is calculating [metric] its own way, the answer you get depends on which one you ask. Same question, different meal.

The semantic layer is how you give every chef the same recipe. dbt lets you define "revenue" or "churn" once, centrally, and broadcast that definition to every tool that needs it. Less variance. Consistent answers. It doesn't matter which chef is working or which tool you ask.

This is what prevents the scenario where your AI and your dashboard disagree. It's not a model problem. It's a definitions problem, and the semantic layer is the fix.

When someone buys an AI product, they're buying the chef, and dbt is the prep kitchen and the recipe book. The recipe book is what stops two chefs from making the same dish two different ways.

05 / how to talk about this

questions that open the room

These are questions from how I actually think about this. They're not scripts. Use them to see how I'd open the conversation, then find your own version. Ask one, then listen.

pull these out as needed
  • "If you get the wrong answer, does that even matter?"
    Start here. If it matters, the conversation shifts to trust and accuracy. If they pause, they haven't thought through the stakes yet. That pause is the real discovery. Softer version: "If you get the wrong answer, what happens?"
  • "What decision actually changes if you get the right answer?"
    Forces them to connect AI output to an actual business outcome. If they can't name one, the use case isn't real yet. If they can, you know exactly what you're solving for.
  • "If AI disappeared tomorrow, would anyone do this the old-fashioned way?"
    Gets at whether the use case has real value or only exists because AI makes it possible. If they'd do it without AI, the outcome matters. If not, it's worth understanding why they're doing it at all.
  • "If you brought in [AI tool] today, how many different versions of [metric] would it see?"
    Let them do the math out loud. Every system they name is another version of the truth. dbt's semantic layer collapses that to one.
  • "If I walked into your org as AI right now, where would I start looking?"
    Makes the input problem real and immediate. If they struggle to answer, that fragmentation is exactly what dbt's transformation layer is built to solve.
  • "Can you trace the answer all the way back, from where the data started to what the AI returned?"
    Input to process to output. If they can't trace it, they can't trust it. dbt's lineage makes that trace possible.
  • "Where does [metric] live in your org right now? How many places is it defined?"
    Diagnostic. Ask it and wait. If they pause or start listing systems, the problem is already visible. The semantic layer is the answer.
  • "If your AI and your dashboard gave two different answers to the same question, would you know why?"
    Most people have been here or are afraid of it. It's a process problem, not a model problem. dbt's semantic layer ensures both read from the same definition.
  • "Who owns the definition of [metric] in your org? What happens when two teams are working from different numbers?"
    Governance question. If nobody owns it, it lives everywhere and means nothing. dbt puts that definition in code, versioned, with a clear owner.

A chef without recipes is just guessing. They might be talented, they might get it right sometimes, but the meal is different every time and nobody can explain why. That's what AI looks like without a context layer. It's reading whatever it can find and doing its best with it.

The context layer is where your org's knowledge lives: what [metric] means, how it's calculated, who owns it, and what can be trusted. That layer needs to exist somewhere. It needs to be consistent. It needs to be shared across every tool that needs it.

That's why it belongs in dbt. Not because dbt is the only tool that can hold a definition, but because dbt is already where your data gets shaped, tested, and made trustworthy. Putting the context layer there means it lives next to the logic that produces the data, not downstream in a BI tool or inside someone's head. It makes the most sense for it to live where the work is already happening.
