ai forces preference

written april 2026 · draft

How I got here

I was visiting my dad recently, and before I left we started talking about AI. He's been going down the rabbit hole: online courses, videos, trying to stay current, because he's worried about being replaced if he doesn't keep up. I don't think he's wrong to pay attention, but I think the concern is pointed at the wrong part of the system.

Refusing to engage with AI is like refusing to use the internet in 1999: you can take that stance, but you'll get left behind. My dad is a sharp guy, industrial engineer by training, went back for his MBA, spent time at GE and IBM in global operations. He's dealt with big systems and real complexity his whole career, but he's never been deep in software the way I have, so I understand where the anxiety lands. Here is how I actually think about it.

For most of history, the process was where all the value in the chain lived.

The Process Was The Value

Leetcode, competitive interviews, prestigious programs, PhDs: people spent careers mastering the craft of execution, and that made sense. When the process was rare and hard to replicate, the person who mastered it became the most important part of the system.

This is why Gordon Ramsay is famous: he mastered Beef Wellington, and the Great British Baking Show exists because people find genuine mastery of craft compelling. When the process is rare, the person who owns it becomes the bottleneck and the most valuable node in the chain.

AI Removes The Process

AI can code now, not just autocomplete: it can design systems, write tests, debug, review pull requests, and do in minutes what used to take a team of ten engineers days. The process stopped being scarce.

When people ask whether AI will take jobs, they're asking about the process, and for a lot of process-focused roles the answer is yes: the drive-through operator, the junior coder, anyone whose job was to execute a well-defined task reliably at scale. That work is going away, not because AI is magic, but because the process stopped being the hard part. What's scarce now is not the ability to build, it's knowing what to build and why: what context do you give it, what makes a good output versus a bad one, what are you actually trying to accomplish? That's judgment, taste, preference.

Worth saying: you should still verify what AI gives you. It gets things wrong, and the outputs are only as good as the context you provide. But models are improving fast, and new ones keep coming. The gap between "impressive" and "reliable" is closing with every release.

Two Analogies
The Lottery

Lottery winners are a useful extreme. Someone spends a lifetime inside a system where work equals earning: you show up, you get paid, the harder you work the more you make. That logic becomes identity. Then overnight the process is gone, and a lot of them fall apart, not because they're bad people but because they've never had to think clearly about what they actually wanted. Without the process to orient around, they don't know what they were working toward. AI is doing this to whole categories of workers right now, just slower.

The Vegan Room

Gordon Ramsay can master Beef Wellington, but present it to a room full of vegans and the craft is irrelevant. Nobody buys it. I don't like Beef Wellington either. The process being excellent doesn't save you if no one wants what it produces. A lot of people spent careers perfecting execution without ever asking whether anyone wanted what they were building, and now that the process is going away, that question is unavoidable.

What You'd Actually Gain

The same pattern plays out at every level. Teams and organizations making AI and data decisions right now are often choosing capabilities based on what they think they're supposed to want, without asking what they'd actually gain.

A team decides they need real-time streaming because real-time sounds right and the vendors pitch it well. But streaming is operationally complex and expensive to maintain. When you trace back to what they're actually trying to accomplish, a nightly batch job would have done the job just as well. The decision got made before anyone asked what the real-time piece would specifically deliver — not in theory, but for their use case, against their actual constraints.

The same thing happens with machine learning. A team spins up a model when a few well-placed business rules would have been faster to build, easier to explain, and cheaper to maintain. Not because ML is wrong for the problem, but because nobody stopped to ask: what would we actually gain from this that we don't already have?

The food system works the same way. Nobody consciously decides they want mechanically separated chicken — the system gets there through pricing, convenience, and gradual rationalization. Once you've made the input seem acceptable, the decision is already made upstream of the evaluation. The same thing happens in technology: once a team decides "we're an AI company" or "we need to be real-time," the tradeoffs stop getting examined. The want runs ahead of the analysis.

The right question isn't "what can AI do?" — it's "what would AI give us that we don't have now, and what would we trade for it?" That question, asked honestly before any build starts, is worth more than any vendor evaluation.

What This Means

With AI doing the process, the bottleneck shifts to inputs and outputs — and that's not abstract. The person who can clearly define what they want to accomplish is now more valuable than the person who can execute it. If you can describe what good looks like, you can direct AI to get there. If you can't, you'll get outputs that technically work but don't solve the actual problem.

A practical test: before your team builds anything with AI, answer two questions. What does a good output look like? And what context does AI need to produce it? If those aren't clear yet, the build shouldn't start. Most failed AI projects aren't model problems — they're definition problems. Nobody agreed on what success looked like before anyone started building.

There's a concrete version of this already playing out in data infrastructure. The semantic layer is a place where teams agree on what outputs mean before anyone builds anything: what does "revenue" mean, what counts as an active user. Before it existed, every team ran their own query and got a different answer. You define what good looks like once, and every downstream consumer — a human query, a dashboard, an AI agent — gets the same answer to the same question. That's what AI requires more broadly: not better prompting, but clearer definitions of what you're actually after.

What changes from here: the divide between engineering decisions and business decisions starts to collapse. That divide existed because the process required specialists. With AI handling the process, one person can work end to end — understanding the inputs, defining the outputs, getting there. Junior engineers ten years from now will ship what senior engineers ship today. The change isn't in what gets built, it's in who can build it and how fast.

Figure Out What You Want

The jobs going away are the beginning, not the end. The harder question follows: what do you actually want to build? Not what you've been trained to produce — what you'd reach for if execution wasn't the obstacle.

Start with the output. What does the thing you want to build look like when it's done? Who benefits, and what changes for them? If you can answer that clearly, you can work backwards to the inputs you need and use AI to handle the process in between. If you can't, that's the problem to solve — not which tool to pick.

Then ask whether the output you want is actually tied to value. Are you building something because it solves a real problem for a specific person or situation, or because it meets a standard you've been told to care about? When AI can handle execution for almost anything, differentiation is entirely in judgment: what to build, why it matters, and whether the output is actually good.

  • If you're in data: spin up dbt Core, point it at something real, and start with a question you actually want answered
  • If you're in design or product: open Figma and try Figma Make — find out what you'd actually build if execution wasn't the blocker
  • If you're evaluating AI for your team: write one paragraph on what good looks like first — what the output is, who it helps, and what you'd trade to get it. That's more useful than any vendor evaluation
  • Define the output you want first, then work backwards to the inputs and the process
  • Develop a point of view on what good looks like — not just the ability to build, but the judgment to know when something is actually working

Preference is a muscle: you get better at knowing what you want by describing the output first, working backwards to the inputs, and noticing when what you got isn't what you actually wanted. That habit, developed now, will matter more than any technical skill AI is already doing for you.
