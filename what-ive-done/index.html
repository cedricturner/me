What I Built

Most of what I build comes down to one thing: it's easier to start from something than from nothing. People push back on a number more easily than they invent one. They react to a diagram faster than they build the mental model. They fill in a template more readily than they face the blank page. The projects below are versions of that move.

Cost of Doing Nothing Calculator. AEs were citing "savings" without numbers, and prospects stalled at "we don't see the value." Interactive model: team size, incident rate, and salary in, annual dollar cost of the legacy setup out. The conversation moves two ways from there. Either the customer confronts what they're already spending, or they push back on the projections, which surfaces the harder numbers we need to build the business case. Now used lightly across the commercial Growth team for early-stage deal justification and pipeline-generation outreach.

Stored Procs vs dbt Visual Explainer. We were explaining stored proc tradeoffs with words and slides, and customers nodded along without actually getting it, which surfaced as rework later. Interactive comparison: stored proc code on one side, the same logic broken into dbt models on the other, then a view showing how the pieces flow through lineage. Once customers could see the breakdown instead of imagining it, dbt clicked. The pattern spread internally too. Multiple SAs adopted it, some built their own spinoffs, and sales directors now pull up the site in intro calls.

PROMPT.md Artifact Replication System. Other SAs saw what I was building with Claude and wanted the same output without spending the time I'd already spent learning how to prompt for it. So I wrote down how I prompt. PROMPT.md is a markdown file you upload at the start of a session, and it gives Claude a working understanding of how I scaffold an artifact, what shape I aim for, and where I usually cut. Drop it in, describe what you want, get a rough draft worth iterating on instead of burning through prompts to find the path. Most of the SA work credited below was built using it.

Workshop-Driven Soft POC Motion. Formal POCs were rigid and slow. Customers had to set up an environment, our team had to support them through it, and the two-week window often ran out before they'd seen half of what they actually needed. Deals dragged. The replacement: multiple short demo sessions in our environment, each one showing a specific feature against a use case the customer cares about. Same outcome, without making them build a validation environment from scratch to find out whether dbt fits. Used across multiple engagements. In commercial, the target is now 80% workshops, 20% hands-on POCs.

Discovery Process Redesign. AEs were walking into discovery without a frame, surfacing the customer's actual architecture and problems halfway through the cycle, when it was too late to redirect. So I built a structured framework around current state, future state, the gap between them, and what closing it enables downstream. AEs now run it on first calls. One of them is doing light technical requirements gathering on his own, getting further into the customer's situation than he was before, and his grasp of the product has grown alongside it. The calls keep landing as follow-ups or stage advances. The pattern has spread beyond SAs, AEs are now mapping current state on their own and walking into discovery with a working picture.
